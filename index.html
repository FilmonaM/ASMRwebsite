WHY ROCK?

"Black music influenced the culture & music of the United States and the world." ‚Äî SoundGirls.org

Rock and roll was born from Black America. From the spirituals and blues to rhythm and gospel, African American musicians created the foundation of American popular music.

By the late 60s, artists like Hendrix used rock as resistance. The distortion was rage. The volume was declaration. The sound was revolution.

REVOLUTIONARY SOUNDS

JIMI HENDRIX

"Purple Haze" (1967)

0:00 0:00

Hendrix embodied the counterculture movement that aligned with Black Power politics. His explosive guitar work and psychedelic rock challenged conventions and authority.

While not explicitly political in lyrics, Hendrix's sonic rebellion and cultural impact made him a symbol of resistance. His connection to the Black Panther Party represented the intersection of musical and political revolution.

Cultural Impact: Hendrix transformed rock into a vehicle for Black expression and counterculture resistance during the height of the Black Power movement.

Rock Music as Resistance

Cultural Power

Black musicians reclaimed rock‚Äîa genre born from their community‚Äîas a tool of liberation.

Sonic Protest

Electric guitars and thunderous drums carried messages that couldn't be silenced or ignored.

Between 1965-1975, Black rock artists weaponized sound itself. When mainstream media could ignore protests, the visceral power of rock forced audiences to confront systemic violence and inspired collective action.

These artists proved rock could be more than entertainment‚Äîit was a lever of change that continues to reverberate through history.
